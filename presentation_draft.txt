Democratised, Decentralised & Distributed Manufacturing

Working draft for show & tell presentation

Communication job

By the end, this audience should understand that CSWG is exploring the coordination infrastructure needed for distributed manufacturing, and that PromiseGrid is one emerging attempt to make commitments, trust, stewardship, and collaboration more explicit across a network of people, organizations, tools, and devices.

Audience assumptions

- They likely already see the limits of centralized manufacturing and brittle supply chains.
- They may understand distributed production socially or politically more than computationally.
- They may not know Promise Theory, PromiseGrid, or why message structure and protocol design matter for manufacturing communities.

Suggested narrative arc

Manufacturing shift -> coordination bottleneck -> Promise Theory lens -> PromiseGrid model -> message example -> current prototypes -> implications and invitation

Suggested slide count

9 to 11 slides for a short show & tell

Slide 1

Title
Distributed manufacturing needs distributed coordination

Visible copy

- Community Systems Working Group
- PromiseGrid and community-operated infrastructure
- FAB26 show & tell

Talk track

Open by making the scope clear. This is not only a talk about where things get made. It is a talk about the infrastructure that helps communities coordinate production, stewardship, and collaboration without recreating centralized control.

Slide 2

Title
The manufacturing shift also changes what infrastructure is needed

Visible copy

- Local production depends on shared knowledge, shared capacity, and shared trust
- Small actors need to cooperate without one actor becoming the platform owner
- The harder problem is often coordination, not fabrication

Talk track

This is the bridge from the workshop theme into your lane. The visible production layer gets attention first, but underneath it there is a coordination layer that often determines whether a distributed system actually works.

Slide 3

Title
CSWG started from a familiar community problem

Visible copy

- Too much hidden infrastructure work
- Too much dependence on a few key organizers or admins
- Too many brittle, siloed, third-party tools
- Goal: reduce burden and increase resilience

Talk track

Keep this grounded. You are not introducing CSWG as a theory project. You are introducing it as a response to recurring coordination pain in communities, makerspaces, non-profits, open-source projects, and small enterprises.

Slide 4

Title
Promise Theory gives us a better way to describe coordination

Visible copy

- An agent can only promise its own behavior
- Requests are not the same as compliance
- Non-commitment is not the same as failure
- Trust is local and built from experience

Talk track

Slow down here. This may be new to many people in the room. The key move is to show that distributed manufacturing already works through promises in practice, whether or not we use that word. Labs, stewards, machines, suppliers, and collaborators all operate through conditional commitments.

Slide 5

Title
ATP and CTP are already promise-shaped ideas

Visible copy

- Available to Promise asks: what supply can actually be committed
- Capable to Promise asks: what production capacity can actually be committed
- PromiseGrid extends that logic across stock, machines, labor, logistics, and collaboration

Talk track

This slide is where Promise Theory becomes legible to a manufacturing audience. You are not replacing familiar supply-chain language. You are showing that ATP and CTP already point toward a world of explicit network commitments.

Slide 6

Title
PromiseGrid asks what a decentralized computer for communities could look like

Visible copy

- Consensus-based computing, communications, and governance
- Users own and operate the grid
- Coordination rules become part of the infrastructure

Talk track

This is your conceptual PromiseGrid slide. Avoid hype. Frame it as an experiment in making coordination, stewardship, and collaboration more explicit and more governable.

Slide 7

Title
A PromiseGrid message is explicit about what is being promised

Visible copy

- Compact form: `grid([42(pCID), payload])`
- Richer form: `grid([42(pCID), parents, payload, proof])`
- `pCID` identifies the protocol
- `payload` carries the promise content
- `parents` and `proof` connect context and verification

Talk track

This is the technical explainer slide. Show the audience that PromiseGrid messages are not vague packets. The structure makes the protocol, the promise content, and sometimes the proof chain visible.

Optional on-slide example

```text
grid([42(pCID), payload])
payload = ["MSG", "gateway-bob", "m4-ivan", 1, "BT-1042", "created"]
```

Talk track

In plain language, this says one participant is making a status promise about an order. That concrete example helps people see that the message structure is part of the coordination model.

Slide 8

Title
The recent prototypes are testing real coordination problems

Visible copy

- Production workflow handoffs
- Storage and compute promises
- Capability tokens and scoped access
- Constrained-device participation
- Shared collaboration and versioned state

Talk track

This is the overview slide before you name concrete lines of work. It tells the audience that PromiseGrid is moving beyond abstract architecture into practical experiments.

Slide 9

Title
Current prototype work spans workflow, devices, and collaboration

Visible copy

- Workflow prototypes: shipping, accounting, device roles, evidence, trust
- Device prototypes: compact radio-visible messages and restart recovery
- Collaboration prototypes: peer retrieval, sync, retention, and Git bridge experiments

Talk track

This is where you briefly summarize the recent POC landscape without drowning people in numbering. If useful, you can mention that the most current collaboration line now includes continuous peer sync, retrieval, retention, and Git bridge work, which makes it feel materially more real.

Slide 10

Title
If this works, local production networks gain more governable infrastructure

Visible copy

- Less dependence on centralized SaaS and hidden gatekeepers
- More visible commitments across organizations and devices
- Better support for stewardship, maintenance, and shared responsibility
- A stronger substrate for collaboration across local production ecosystems

Talk track

This is the implication slide. Connect back to fablabs, partner sites, repair cultures, distributed production, and the long-term need for shared coordination without uniform ownership.

Slide 11

Title
We are looking for collaborators, critics, and early testers

Visible copy

- Follow the work
- Share use cases and design questions
- Help test prototypes as they emerge
- Help shape infrastructure that communities can actually govern

Talk track

End with an invitation rather than a conclusion. The right closing note is: this is active work, it is not finished, and we want grounded manufacturing communities to shape it.

Positioning relative to the other presenters

- Internet of Production can speak to networked production ecosystems at a broader systems level.
- Mechanisms Community can speak to community-centered production, local resilience, and values.
- Your lane is the coordination substrate: what kinds of digital systems let those ecosystems cooperate without recreating central platform dependence.

Useful transitions

- "The visible production network depends on a less visible coordination network."
- "That is where CSWG has been focusing its effort."
- "Promise Theory gives us a useful vocabulary for that layer."
- "PromiseGrid is one attempt to build infrastructure around that logic."
- "To see why that matters, it helps to look at what an actual message looks like."
- "The important point is not the syntax by itself. It is the legibility of the commitment."
- "What is becoming clearer now is that the prototypes are testing real coordination seams."

Editing guidance for delivery

- Keep Promise Theory human-readable and concrete.
- Use ATP and CTP as bridges, not digressions.
- Do not overclaim deployment readiness.
- Treat POC18 as active and materially advancing, but still experimental.
- Favor "community-operated," "shared coordination," "stewardship," and "governable infrastructure" over abstract decentralization slogans.
